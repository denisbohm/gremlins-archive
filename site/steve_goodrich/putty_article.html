Putty
by Steve Goodrich
o come up with the most tried and true article on the subject of fillers and epoxy putties, we must first address two subjects.

The first being that we will assume one wishes to read about products which do not crack nor "unglue" from the kit itself after ten or fifteen years. We must assume one is looking for products which will have no known life and virtually last forever.

Second, we must assume that one realizes "one size does not fit all." We need to match the filler/putty to the material of the kit itself. Otherwise, we will be headed for disaster from the word go.

First up, lets deal with the oldest of the three mediums for kits; styrene. This is the hard plastic which is injected into molds creating "trees" from which we clip parts. This is the hard plastic we all know from the aurora kits.

Filler for this medium should never, ever be anything but fillers that come in tubes such as Squadron Green! This volatile material actually attacks the styrene... melts it to an extent and mates well. Drawbacks on these fillers are that they dry slowly and shrink. This later point means you may need to add a layer or two once curing is accomplished.

Properly cured (overnight) Squadron Green type "tube putties" sound very easy, but these type of fillers should only be use on styrene and nothing else! Used on all vinyl kits and some resins; these two materials have a tendency to move on a molecular to obviously level over the years.

Tube fillers, while held in place by paint to these materials, do not bond with them and will crack over time. Tube fillers, unless bonded with styrene, cure hard and brittle.

Vinyl kits, especially, need a putty which locks in with the piece. If you can't make your present A.B. epoxy pass the following tests, scrap it!

Mix parts A and B (a small dab) according to package directions. Let it cure for about a week. Now, test this dab (say a small roll or "snake"). Does it bend or does it break? If it snaps rather than bending a bit before it snaps, throw it in the trash.

Next take a waste rim from both hard and soft vinyl: Extreme end of hard would be a Horizon kit and the extreme end of soft would be the nearly flesh like quality: ie. Halcyon's Facehugger. The vinyl of Screamin' kits is a happy medium.

Mix a small wad of your present A.B. epoxy and press it onto a scrap of prewashed and dried vinyl. The following day, if it can be removed by bending the vinyl or peeling it off, try this again using a few dots of super glue on the waste piece of vinyl. If this second experiment fails to bond the plastic to putty...loose the putty!

There are two A.B. epoxy putties that pass the tests which weren't actually developed for this hobby and they are both user friendly and pass all environ- mental tests of not producing lethal fumes nor curing reactions.

The first is called Kneadatite, or "green". This is a ribbon of yellow and blue. When you clip off a section and knead it together, it becomes green. (massive surprise here!)

This putty has amazing stick! So much so that one must keep rubbing tools or brush handle (use the latter to roll out the edges, like a rolling pin, for flat surfaces) against the side of the nose. This putty will stick to anything and has an amazing "flex". (anything meaning a plastic surface prewashed)

Rubbing your sculpting tool aside your nose from time to time, picks up a thin layer of skin oil that repels the "green". Otherwise, "green" will cling to your tool. If this method seems gross, consider that dipping a tool in cooking oil, will create too much of an oily problem. "Green" does not respect water and there is no better way to control this putty than with what God has endowed us with.

Working time of "green" is from thirty to sixty minutes, depending on the size of the mix. If you can fill and sculpt what you desire in five minutes, you can feel free to prime and/or paint right over "green". If has a chemical cure, or "kick", and is not affected by things like water, or air dry time. (in fact, it will “kick” under water) So, paint away over it.

"Green", after it fully cures, can also be dremeled, filed and sanded down to the point of fine wet/dry sanding of any other A.B. epoxies.

Product number two from Polymeric is their literally outrageous A.B. epoxy called Repair-it-quick. This is presently sold through "New Pig" exclusively as "Pig Putty". But outrageous is the only superlative that can describe this material!

It is used like all A.B. epoxies, yet cures concrete hard with still a bit of flex. Water is used to smooth out rather than oil. It is especially sublime to build up missing teeth or claws which frequently fail to fill out in resin.

Drawback is that it cures fast. With a pea sized mix, one has only about three minutes to work. You can quickly work up small area of filling and count on Pig Putty sanding or filing very well.
